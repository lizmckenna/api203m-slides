Writing High Quality Notes (Lareau)

Lareau (Chapter 6): “The goal of writing field notes is to convey, using words, your experience so that others may experience too, albeit in a different fashion, as you show them your data.” (pp. 194)

W.R.I.T.E.

  • W: Who, what, when, where, how
  • R: Reaction (responses to questions and to responses)
  • I: Inaction (silence or non-verbal responses)
  • T: Timing (speed of speaking; joking)
  • E: Emotions (non-verbal signs of emotion)

Assignment #2: Reminders

No penalty for “unproductive” observations

You will not be penalized for conducting an observation that, for whatever reason, does not yield a plethora of direct insights into your policy puzzle. You can reflect on why this happened as part of the assignment.

Purpose: Practice the technique

The spirit and purpose of the assignment is to practice the technique, learn to write rich/thick field notes, and reflect on the method’s strengths and drawbacks.

Why interview?

MEANING

  • →Understand the meaning your respondents attach to life’s events and experiences
  • →“How did that make you feel?”
  • →“Digging deep” to find how a person’s unique experience sheds light on your puzzle/research question

Lareau, Chapter 5

Why interview?

Causal Mechanisms

Identify causal mechanisms and trace processes that are not evident in other forms of data → theory building

Cohen (2016): What does it mean for sexual violence to be a “tool of war” from the perspective of the armed fighters?

Survey Design

Aid in survey design and interpretation

Jung and Cohen (2020): Survey showed that many people in Port-au-Prince receive basic services from gangs. How does this affect perceptions of the gangs’ legitimacy vs. the state, NGOs, the UN, church groups?

Other reasons

  • → You want to get very detailed information
  • → You anticipate wanting to ask follow-up questions based on responses
  • → You plan to ask questions that require lengthy explanation
  • → You are studying a complex or potentially confusing topic, making it difficult to construct a one-size-fits-all survey
  • → You are studying processes, such as how people make decisions

Example guide from Listening to People

Background and Experience

1. Tell me about the family in which you grew up.

a. Probes: What did your parent(s) do for a living? What was life at home like? What do your sisters or brothers do? Tell me about the schools you attended. What did you do outside of school? Who were your friends, or, who did you spend a lot of time with?

LogisticsInterviewingYour Interview

Cultural Knowledge and Mobility

3. Thinking about your current situation, in what ways do you feel you belong and do not belong? Can you describe a moment when you felt confident at work? Can you describe a moment when you weren’t confident?

a. Probes: when, where, what situation, who with, how felt, how felt in body

4. We are interested in learning more about how things are different for people from the way that they grew up and the way things are now.

a. Some people tell us that they notice differences between how people in their family talk about themselves and present themselves compared to how people do this in their current world. Have you noticed any differences in this area or not so much?

Why avoid asking “why?”

Have you ever noticed this: That people never answer what you say? They answer what you mean, or what they think you mean.

Suppose one lady says to another in a country house: ‘Is anybody staying with you?’ The lady does not answer: ‘Yes, the butler, the three footmen, the parlor maid, and so on,’ though the parlor maid may be in the room, or the butler behind her chair.

She says: ‘There is nobody staying with us,’ meaning nobody of the sort you mean…
— Chesterton (1911)
28
LogisticsInterviewingYour Interview

Why avoid asking “why?”

…But suppose a doctor inquiring into an epidemic asks, ‘Who is staying in the house?’ then the lady will remember the butler, the parlor maid, and the rest.

All language is used like that; you never get a question answered literally, even when you get it answered truly.
— Chesterton (1911)

Assignment #3: Interview

Due Wed. April 1

A. Recruit an interview subject and obtain their consent to record the interview.

B. Write an interview guide.

  • –Limit to a realistic number of questions for a 60-minute interview
  • –Include probes to elicit more details
  • –You may want close-ended questions for standard demographic data

C. Conduct, record, and transcribe the interview. Use Zoom, Otter AI, Rev.com, Trint, Whisper, or Happy Scribe (non-English). Translate if not in English.

A note about who to interview

Because we are learning and practicing a new method, students should generally not collect data from vulnerable populations, individuals in unstable contexts, or about very sensitive topics.

→ Instead, consider collecting data from issue area experts or individuals who work with vulnerable people (fellow MPPs could be great!)

ABSOLUTELY NO children, incarcerated people, or individuals with diminished mental capacity. These are protected categories that require extensive ethical review.

Prepping your interview: Consent

Consent script checklist

  • •Who are you?
  • •Why are you doing this?
  • •Benefits and risks?
    • –It’s ok to refuse!
  • •Ok to record?
  • •Ok to cite/quote?
You must find at least one interviewee who agrees to be recorded!

Example script

Thank you for responding to my email invitation for an interview. My name is ______ and I am a graduate student at Harvard conducting this interview for my coursework in a qualitative methods class.

The research is being conducted to explore ___________. Your participation will only be needed once for this interview which should last no more than _________.

I will not be providing any compensation for this interview. You can decline to proceed and you can stop the interview at any time.

With your permission, I’d like to record. Is that okay?
