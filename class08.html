Michelle Fausto

Why are eligible households in California failing to enroll in CalFresh?

DebriefWorkshopAnalysisFocus Groups

Michelle: So, you, uh, actually touched on a lot of the questions I had, which reflects your deep expertise and lived experience in this issue. I do want to go back to the most recent thing that you addressed, that hesitancy among applicants. Did you see that same kind of pattern with the people you worked with at the North Texas Food Bank in terms of being afraid of taking away the benefit from someone else, potentially concerns related to public charge and fears around immigration? And then my other question was—actually, I’ll let you answer that and then I’ll follow up with my other question.

Interviewee: So thank you. The public charge is what I was referring to earlier. So yes, the public charge, the dynamics stayed the same. So, in my undergrad, children that were eligible because either they had a visa or citizenship status didn’t want to impose public charge on their parents, which it was evident that that would not happen if they were applying in their own family unit. Now, applying in the community through the North Texas Food Bank, this issue was very evident, but now from the perspective of the caregiver. So, um, among undocumented individuals especially, it was not uncommon that mostly mothers or women were the ones applying on behalf of the household. I think that is something that I saw immediately—the paperwork, being there in office, doing the documentation, talking to somebody. And again, this is going to be from my perspective that around 95% head of households applying were women.

Eliciting concrete examples can be hard!

Alexa: Can you think of a moment—without sharing anything sensitive—where you had to make a decision under uncertainty? What did that actually look like?

Interviewee: I think from the outside, it can feel like decisions are very clear-cut, or that there is, like, an obvious right answer that people just failed to choose. But in reality, you are often dealing with incomplete information, competing priorities, and very real time pressure. So it is not just about making the “right” decision—it is about making the best possible decision given the constraints…And those constraints are not just informational, they are also institutional, political, sometimes even cultural.

Citing interview data in text

Avoid journalistic terms: “on background,” “off the record,” and “not for attribution” are journalistic terms of art. Avoid them!

Interviewees are typically agreeing to be quoted/paraphrased and cited explicitly or anonymously. Always be clear in your consenting process.

All direct quotations and paraphrased comments need a footnote!

Explicit citation

Power, Samantha, author interview, January 20, 2023, Cambridge, MA.

Anonymous citation

Former UN official, author interview, January 20, 2023, Cambridge, MA.

What is a focus group?

Definition

Heise & Ellsberg (2005)

  • •Group of ~6–10 individuals selected because they share relevant characteristics (concerted recruitment)
  • •Trained moderator
  • •Note-takers & recording (with consent)
  • •Question protocol AND activities

Key Feature

“A research technique that collects data through group interaction on a topic determined by the researcher.”

— Morgan 1996, 130, quoted in Cyr
