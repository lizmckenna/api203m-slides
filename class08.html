Example 1: Nate Powell

Nate: So recentering on the experiences you have either solo or with friends or with Tim, like with the rowing club, what sights, sounds, feelings stand out in those experiences when you visualize them? You could take some time to think about this one, too, to build the image in your mind if you want.

Interviewee: Yeah. I mean, for rowing, there’s a lot of sound feedback, I guess. The more you row, the more you get feedback from the sound of the boat in the water and the way the blade hits the water. So that’s a huge piece of it… when you’re rowing, the worst thing about rowing is when it’s windy. It makes the whole experience terrible… But as a person, I love wind. I think it’s one of my favorite types of weather… The last time we went to Prospect Park, and we were just sitting there, and it happened to be a really windy day, but it was warm, and we were just sitting there, and we had an almost impromptu meditation where we were just sitting there with our eyes closed and just feeling and listening to the wind, and it was so nice.

Example 2: Olivia Mallory

Olivia: Will you tell me about your decision to go to med school?

Noah: “My decision to go to med school I think started really long before I consciously knew it was something that I wanted to do… My father had a bad brain aneurysm, a traumatic bleed in his head when I was in high school and that was a really formative interaction… I was drawn to both the acuity of the care, the formation that it had on my family and myself and really the importance and the significance that the doctors had in such a large and memorable moment…”

Olivia: “Really interesting. And you mentioned in high school and college you continued to be drawn to those moments. Can you bring me to one of those moments in high school or college where you again felt drawn to medicine?”

Organizing interview details

Cohen (2016) — East Timor, 2012

Systematic tracking of every interview:

  • •Date, name/description, purpose
  • •Language, sex, ex-combatant status
  • •Interpreter (and budget for interpreter)
  • •Assigned number for anonymous interviewees

Reporting: Methods Appendix

See Moseley, Chapter 4 for best practices on documenting your interview methodology

Citing interview data in text

Avoid journalistic terms: “on background,” “off the record,” and “not for attribution” are journalistic terms of art. Avoid them!

Interviewees are typically agreeing to be quoted/paraphrased and cited explicitly or anonymously. Always be clear in your consenting process.

All direct quotations and paraphrased comments need a footnote!

Explicit citation

Power, Samantha, author interview, January 20, 2023, Cambridge, MA.

Anonymous citation

Former UN official, author interview, January 20, 2023, Cambridge, MA.

Analyzing interview data (review)

  • →Re-listen & close read — immerse yourself in the data
  • →Memo — write analytical notes as you go
  • →Narrative analysis — how do respondents construct stories from personal experience? Look at chronology, plot, character. Compare and contrast different stories; look for interpretations.
  • →Code your data — identify patterns across transcripts, then larger themes across different sources of qualitative data
  • →Do more interviews! — PAEs: 10–30 interviews. Continue until “saturation”

What is a focus group?

Definition

Heise & Ellsberg (2005)

  • •Group of ~6–10 individuals selected because they share relevant characteristics (concerted recruitment)
  • •Trained moderator
  • •Note-takers & recording (with consent)
  • •Question protocol AND activities

Key Feature

“A research technique that collects data through group interaction on a topic determined by the researcher.”

— Morgan 1996, 130, quoted in Cyr
