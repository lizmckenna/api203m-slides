Organizing interview details

Cohen (2016) — East Timor, 2012

Systematic tracking of every interview:

  • •Date, name/description, purpose
  • •Language, sex, ex-combatant status
  • •Interpreter (and budget for interpreter)
  • •Assigned number for anonymous interviewees

Reporting: Methods Appendix

See Moseley, Chapter 4 for best practices on documenting your interview methodology

Citing interview data in text

Avoid journalistic terms: “on background,” “off the record,” and “not for attribution” are journalistic terms of art. Avoid them!

Interviewees are typically agreeing to be quoted/paraphrased and cited explicitly or anonymously. Always be clear in your consenting process.

All direct quotations and paraphrased comments need a footnote!

Explicit citation

Power, Samantha, author interview, January 20, 2023, Cambridge, MA.

Anonymous citation

Former UN official, author interview, January 20, 2023, Cambridge, MA.

Analyzing interview data (review)

  • →Re-listen & close read — immerse yourself in the data
  • →Memo — write analytical notes as you go
  • →Narrative analysis — how do respondents construct stories from personal experience? Look at chronology, plot, character. Compare and contrast different stories; look for interpretations.
  • →Code your data — identify patterns across transcripts, then larger themes across different sources of qualitative data
  • →Do more interviews! — PAEs: 10–30 interviews. Continue until “saturation”

What is a focus group?

Definition

Heise & Ellsberg (2005)

  • •Group of ~6–10 individuals selected because they share relevant characteristics (concerted recruitment)
  • •Trained moderator
  • •Note-takers & recording (with consent)
  • •Question protocol AND activities

Key Feature

“A research technique that collects data through group interaction on a topic determined by the researcher.”

— Morgan 1996, 130, quoted in Cyr
