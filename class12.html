Qual research: why you should care

Qualitative research is a type of research that explores and provides deep, palpable insights into real-world problems.

Kara Chyung — CareerLink Job Assistance Event, North Philadelphia Library

Real-world problem: Unemployment services and how institutions serve structurally disadvantaged communities.

12
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment
As I walk past the front of the library, a man is lying across the steps, his face obscured by his black sweatshirt and his bare feet and ankles gray with dirt. A grocery shopping cart positioned lopsidedly next to him holds a child-size tricycle. [...] As I walk into the main library room, I am immediately struck by the architecture: intricately carved columns and panels line the ivory walls and ceiling. The room is vast, with tall ceilings. Golden chandeliers descend from the ceiling. [...] Button Down Man sits leaning forward slightly, with his elbows resting on his knees. His laptop sits slightly to the side, so that it is not directly between them. He maintains eye contact with Sparkly Bag Client and talks in a soft but confident tone.
13
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment
What a survey or quantitative variable would have captured: This young woman is unemployed, expecting, lacks a degree, and is seeking services. It could code her as a “CareerLink participant” in an administrative dataset. A regression could estimate the effect of degree-requirement removal on hiring rates for people like her.
What that would not capture: Why the gap between policy and outcome persists. The field notes suggest some potential mechanisms: the counselors are warm, competent, and caring, and they still can’t place her.
  • The system is designed to serve everyone (a pregnant teenager, an aging veteran, a mother doing homework with her kids), which means it’s designed to serve no one in particular and mechanisms are heterogeneous (age, loneliness, digital literacy for elderly veteran)
14
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment

You also can’t survey the moment she says her resume might not “count,” or the silence after “Did you say babies?”, or the way she clutches her bag the entire time and doesn’t shift in her seat once.

(Those details tell you something about the felt experience of navigating the system in a way that quant research cannot)

15
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment

Qualitative research goes beyond surface-level observations to uncover motivations, beliefs, and lived experiences. It allows you to step into the shoes of the people you are working with or for, to understand the world from their perspective.

Michelle Fausto — Interview with a SNAP Case Navigator

Real-world problem: Why does California have a comparatively low CalFresh enrollment rate among people who are eligible?

The interviewee occupies two pairs of shoes at once and Michelle had the instinct to ask about both. At 01:04, the interviewee starts with her professional bio. At 01:37, Michelle asks: “Can I step us back for a moment and, if you feel comfortable in answering, ask about your childhood involvement with SNAP?”

16
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment
“As a kid, I vividly remember going to the health and human services office in El Monte, California and going in and, like, being stripped down as a child of, like, maybe I had a toy in my pocket. This was so dehumanizing. I’m sure my mom was already feeling some type of way bringing me here, her kid after school when she wished she would just be home, sitting in uncomfortable chairs in a place that it’s, like, dead silent. People don’t even want to look at each other in the eye.”

When she describes meeting families at grocery stores and WIC offices instead of making them come to a government building, she seems to be describing what she wished had existed for her mother.

17
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment
What a survey or quantitative variable could capture:

SNAP enrollment data can tell you that California’s participation rate among eligible households is lower than the national average. Administrative records can tell you how many applications were started, denied, or abandoned. A regression could estimate the effect of application complexity on completion rates, or the effect of public charge rhetoric on enrollment among immigrant households.

But none of that would tell you:

Why the gap persists despite the program being designed to serve exactly these families. Nor would they have produced the interviewee’s dual perspective (from the child’s POV and the bureaucratic mechanics that she sees from the navigator’s desk).

18
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment

The interview surfaces layer after layer of why

  • →Fear as a rational calculation, not ignorance. Undocumented mothers (95% of the HOH she works with) refuse to apply because they believe it will trigger public charge consequences for their immigration cases. Even after months of trust-building, “other families just didn’t think the risk was worth it and we respected that.”
  • →Shame as community enforcement. People “frankly didn’t want to be seen coming out of a health and human services building… walking out of that building felt like you labeled yourself, you gave yourself an A on your chest.”
19
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment
The verification interviews felt like interrogation. “When applicants miss the call, because they’re at work, because the number is undisclosed, because they’re afraid of unknown callers, they have to start over. ‘You did everything right!’ It’s just a phone call. I’m so sorry that we have to start all over again.”
…That’s what qualitative research does that nothing else can:

it lets you understand a system from the perspective of someone who lives inside it.

Creativity & New Frontiers

There’s room for creativity

James’ point: The best qualitative work doesn’t just follow a template — it finds inventive ways to answer the question. The methods you choose should fit the world you’re studying, not the other way around.

Acknowledging limitations honestly

Final Assignment

Mock PAE Proposal

  • →What is it? — A research proposal that identifies a policy-relevant question and designs a qualitative or mixed-methods study to answer it
  • →Why a proposal? — Writing a proposal forces you to connect question → method → data → analysis — the full pipeline we’ve practiced all semester
26
Guest LectureDebriefTakeawaysFinal Assignment

Rubric Highlights

Research Question

Clear, focused, policy-relevant, and answerable with qualitative or mixed methods

Methods Design

Appropriate method(s) for the question, with justified sampling, recruitment, and data collection plan

Analysis Plan

Coding strategy, analytical framework, and how you’ll move from data to findings

Ethics

IRB considerations, informed consent, positionality, and potential harms

Limitations

Honest assessment of trade-offs and what the design cannot tell us

Tips & Examples
