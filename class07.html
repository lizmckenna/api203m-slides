Some Common Problems
(and How to Troubleshoot)

Logistical hurdles
  • Recruitment: persistence; warm introductions
  • Confirmation calls/emails
  • Run multiple recorders
  • Good etiquette (especially helpful for snowball sampling and goodwill for future referrals/intros)
Getting to your key themes
  • Pare question guide down to essentials
  • Plan your probes
  • Consider “the list” technique (pp. 80–1) in Listening to People

Theory

Troubleshooting Common Problems

Answers are vague
  • Ask about a specific action, event, or experience (“tell me about a time when”)
  • Stay with a question long enough
  • General question followed by probe (vs. starting with narrow question)
Power inequalities
  • Acknowledge shared/similar experiences
  • Dress, etc. are important signals of who you are
  • Listen closely & respond sensitively
  • Always remember principles of ethical research — follow letter & spirit of informed consent

Lareau, Ch. 5

Theory

Troubleshooting Common Problems

Overly talkative respondent
  • Intervene early to set the tone
  • Repeat your question if response is off-track
  • Examine & debrief your own transcripts
Overly succinct respondent
  • Intervene early to set the tone
  • Consider stopping or diverting from your line of questions to build rapport
  • Allow respondent to elaborate on something they care about even if it is off-track

Lareau, Ch. 5

Spot the errors in this excerpt from a transcript of an interview by Prof. Liz McKenna
(There are at least three!)

EMAnd, why might you and Tram differ on that question?
JLWhy? I don’t know how. It—
EMOr, what is the difference, a—
JLHow? I can tell you how—
EMThe how, yeah.
JL[inaudible 00:04:41] I can’t read her mind, and keep [inaudible 00:04:43]. But, it’s like—
Error #1: “Why” questions “And, why might you and Tram differ…” — A “why” question in an interview is rarely a good idea. Invites defensiveness, assumes a simple reason behind often complex actions. Responses are often vague/philosophical. “What/how” questions are more likely to elicit storytelling.
Error #2: Speculating on others “…why might you and Tram differ…” — Do not ask the interviewee to speculate on someone else’s state of mind. It does not yield good data.
Error #3: “Inaudible” portions “[inaudible 00:04:41]… [inaudible 00:04:43]” — Sometimes the transcript will return “inaudible” portions. It’s your job to go back, re-listen, and clean up the transcript — remember it is your valuable primary data!

Interview Excerpt — Wei Wei Liu

What has led to many students coming to HKS wanting to work on critical social and political issues but often ending up in industries such as consulting?

WL: How did you end up with McKinsey and what did you want to do when you came into HKS?

Interviewee: Well, I don’t actually know (laugh). It was kinda unexpected. As you know that I was selected by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) right after my undergrad and due to some weird circumstances, I could not take up the post. After that I came to HKS but I don’t actually have a clear goal in mind in terms of what to do next, instead of going to the MFA.

I know that I want to eventually go back to China as all my family are there and if I’m not going to the MFA, I will have to go through some other organizations. Consulting is considered a good choice: prestigious, pays well, and allows me to go back. You know, there aren’t many prestigious NGOs that are doing well or making big impacts. So I’m not planning to do that in China either. Apart from that, I also don’t know what else I could do.

Interviewee: McKinsey and other consulting firms have at least some clear structures to recruitment. I went for their info session and talked to friends who were in consulting. WL: Hahaha. okay, I was a consultant too, as you know. But people in HKS often half-jokingly say that we “sell our souls” to the capitalist world. What do you think of that?

But seriously, I don’t necessarily think we are “selling our souls.” There are some really cool projects that McKinsey does in China and some of them are also very impactful. I will try to get on those projects if possible. Again, I think it is probably the best for myself to explore various industries first. I didn’t really have work experience before coming to HKS so I cannot fully commit myself to anything just yet. Probably McKinsey will help me figure a way forward, too. I don’t think it is a horrible idea, to be honest.

What is a code ?

“Tags or labels for assigning units of meaning …usually attached to ‘chunks’ of varying size: words, phrases, sentences, or even whole paragraphs.”
Miles and Huberman (1994)
17
ChallengesExcerptAnalysisExamplesTips

What is a code ?

[Assigns] a summative or evocative attribute for a portion of qualitative data.
Saldaña (2021)
18
ChallengesExcerptAnalysisExamplesTips

What Is Qualitative Coding?

→Coding is the analytical workhorse for qualitative research

→Applying labels (or “codes”) to your data in order to find patterns across participants, evidence of interesting processes at play, and broader takeaways from the research

→Codes are the middle step that take us from raw data to a set of findings

19
ChallengesExcerptAnalysisExamplesTips

Why code ?

Coding is the “critical link” between data collection and their explanation of meaning.
Charmaz (2001)

ChallengesExcerptAnalysisExamplesTips

Coding Is Creative

→You can use software, but it is not fundamentally a mechanical or technical exercise

→It is a dynamic, intuitive and creative process of:

  • Inductive reasoning
  • Thinking
  • Theorizing

Basit (2003)

ChallengesExcerptAnalysisExamplesTips

What Makes a Good Code?

  • +“Essence-capturing”
  • +Research-question dependent
  • +Generative codes are often:
  • Descriptive of the phenomenon
  • Identify causal conditions
  • Strategies
  • +Thoroughness
  • Memo writing
  • Cross-referencing w/ literature review
  • Constantly referring back to research question
  • +Iterative — rarely will we get coding “right” on a first pass

A Strong Interview Submission (Assignment 3)

→Interviews that elicit vivid stories (e.g., in response to “can you give me an example of that” or “tell me about a time when”) vs. abstract statements

→Good probing (i.e., reflecting back what the interviewee said to follow up on a thread, asking for more detail vs. just moving on from question to question)

  • A quick way to scan for this: read the succession of interviewer timestamped questions. Does it seem like you are just reading off your protocol, or responding to what the interviewee is saying in a deeper way?
  • What Lareau calls “staying with a question long enough” to get the detail needed to produce good interview data

→Eliciting the meaning that the interviewee attaches to their experiences (cognitive empathy)

A Weaker Submission (Assignment 3)

  • →Interview overly succinct (e.g., only a few pages of transcript). This will be the main reason for points deduction — a submission that does not reflect a full 60-minute interview.
  • →Evidence of lack of rapport; just running through questions with little engagement
  • →Lots of leading or double-barreled questions
  • →Many “why” questions
  • →Little probing for concrete examples, stories or anecdotes
  • →Asking the interviewee to speculate about someone else’s state of mind

ChallengesExcerptAnalysisExamplesTips

Memo Assessment (Assignment 3)

The memo (250–500 words) should describe how you chose your interview subject, what you experienced as the benefits and drawbacks of an in-depth, semi-structured interview, and reflections on any surprises, substantive questions, or emergent findings related to your research puzzle that arose in the process of conducting the interview (consider some of the analytic approaches we discussed in class today; but no need to formally code).
  • →A strong memo shows awareness of / analyzes minor weaknesses in your interview transcript in the memo (e.g., I should have probed more at XX:XX when interviewee said Y; I asked a double-barrelled question at XX:XX)
Remember to include the whole transcript, like the “introductory” or “small talk” components of the interview that were recorded, as well as the consent (if possible). We want to see the whole transcript (and please proofread it to make sure it is legible).
