Matvey Anufriev

Since the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, public opinion polls in Russia have registered consistently high and stable approval of the actions of the Russian Armed Forces in Ukraine. Levada Center polling data shows approval 78% in 09/2025, 76% in 09/2024, 73% in 09/2023, 74% in 09/2022. Similar levels of support have been registered by other pollsters when respondents were asked a general question about approval of the war.

3
PuzzlesEthicsApplicationLooking Ahead

Matvey Anufriev (cont.)

However, Levada’s polling data shows a completely different picture when survey questions are formulated differently. When asked whether peace negotiations should begin, 62% express support in 2025 – up from c.50% in 2023. When asked whether you would support Putin’s decision to stop the war, 80% demonstrate support in 2025, up from 62% in 2023.

The current political environment of state propaganda and repression affect the reliability of public opinion and survey data. In authoritarian regimes, preference falsification is a common phenomenon. Qualitative research can supplement polling data to confirm whether preference falsification is present and to determine why the majority of Russians approve actions of its military in Ukraine while simultaneously supporting peace negotiations and stopping the war.

In 2024, results showed that student performance in every state was still below pre-pandemic levels, with a growing gap between higher and lower achieving students. Out of the bottom 25% in achievement, more than two-thirds are lower-income, with 25% being Black and more than 40% being Hispanic.

With qualitative studies, I am curious as to why free community and educational resources, such as public libraries, academically benefit certain demographic groups differently — and specifically, why there may be greater or lower participation among certain communities.

Joe Calvi

The Populace Insights: Private Opinion in America (2025) study measured how Americans’ expressed beliefs about sensitive political and social issues differ publicly and privately. Using a list experiment with over 3,000 U.S. adults, the study found that 14% publicly said they trust the government to tell the truth, whereas only 6% privately agreed — suggesting that even low levels of institutional trust expressed in surveys may be exaggerated.

Joe Calvi (cont.)

A natural follow-up question is why Americans distrust government so deeply and how are those attitudes formed?

Qualitative research could explore how government behavior, political messaging, and personal experiences with government institutions shape how individuals interpret and respond to government action. In-depth interviews or focus groups could reveal the lived experiences behind the data — examining how government authority is perceived through narratives, experiences, and meanings.

Ethics in qual and quant research

QUANT
  • •Ensuring subjects fully understand what they are consenting to (esp. experiments, large-N surveys)
  • •Large datasets with personal identifiers pose risks of re-identification, even if anonymized
  • •Data storage, security, and sharing, particularly with sensitive topics like health or criminal justice
QUAL
  • •Power dynamics (Ayrton)
  • •Protecting confidentiality (personal narratives, interviews, detailed interactions)
  • •Researchers’ relationships with subjects (emotional distress, coercion, unintended consequences)
  • •Retraumatization / exploitation of subjects for personal gain

What is “human subjects research”?

HHS Regulation 45 CFR 46

Definition

A human subject is a living individual about whom an investigator (whether professional or student) conducting research…

Criterion 1

Obtains information or biospecimens through intervention or interaction with the individual, and uses, studies, or analyzes the information or biospecimens

Criterion 2

Obtains, uses, studies, analyzes, or generates identifiable private information or identifiable biospecimens

Scenario 1: Can I quote you on that?

A group of students conducted interviews with employees and former employees of a major social media company.

One interviewee made an outrageous comment that students saw as a unique, key piece of evidence, central to their argument in their PAE. After the interview, the interviewee contacted the students to request that they not use his comment.

One of the students—a former journalist—felt that the initial comment was made “on the record” and couldn’t simply be deleted or forgotten. Other members of the group felt uncomfortable using a quotation from someone who explicitly requested it not be used.

21
PuzzlesEthicsApplicationLooking Ahead

Suggested answer: NO

  • →Informed consent is based on trust and may be withdrawn by a research participant at any time
  • →In research, unlike journalism, harm (including potential reputational harm) must be avoided
  • →The respondent objected to the use of the quote

How should we think about informed consent in this case?

  • Informed consent includes a respondent’s right to have their responses treated anonymously and in a manner that will not harm them.
  • Respondents also have the right to not reply to any particular question or to stop the interview at any time.
  • It is a continuous process; consent may be withdrawn much later.

Scenario 2: Conversing with Combatants

A student pair proposes to travel to a country currently experiencing conflict in order to interview current and former fighters. They have never been to that country before, and neither speaks the relevant languages.

They hope to find a translator and a driver once they arrive through a contact at the US embassy. They will only have one week to spend in the country, and don’t yet have any direct contacts with armed groups on the ground.

Should this project continue as planned? If not, what other options might the students pursue?

How should we think about ethics in this case?

This project will be almost impossible to conduct in an ethical manner as designed. Students should change the research design:

Examine existing records of interview data from others

Interview NGO and U.N. staff who have experience with the conflict

Use Zoom interviews to interview local experts

Think creatively about gathering other forms of data (e.g., social media posts)

Ethical considerations for your observation assignment

Public settings only

In ethnographies of private settings, obtain consent from individuals/groups being studied

Anonymize data

Field notes and memo should not contain identifying information unless essential and consent has been given

Non-intervention

As much as possible, observe the natural course of events

Sensitivity to context

Some behaviors might be considered more private, even in a public setting

Prepping your interview: consent

You must find at least one interviewee who agrees to be recorded for this class!

Prepare a consent script for the beginning of your interview:

  • Who are you?
  • Why are you doing this?
  • What are the benefits and risks?
  • It’s ok to refuse to do this!
  • Ok to record?
  • Ok to cite/quote, by name or anonymously?

Thank you for responding to my email invitation for an interview. My name is ______ and I am a graduate student at Harvard conducting this study for my coursework.

The research is being conducted to explore ___________. Your participation will only be needed once for this interview which should last no more than _________.

I will not be providing any compensation for this interview. You can decline to proceed and you can stop the interview at any time.

With your permission, I’d like to record. Is that okay?

Ethical considerations for community-engaged research

Reciprocity and mutual benefit

Collaborations should offer clear benefits to all parties involved, respect community knowledge, and ensure outcomes address community needs

Transparency and accountability

Research goals, methods, findings, check-ins, updates w/ all stakeholders should be transparent

Informed consent

Participants must understand and voluntarily agree to the research process

Knowledge co-creation

Encourage active participation from those directly impacted by the research (design, data collection, analysis, dissemination, recognition)
