Ethnography & Observation

What is ethnography?

→Researcher is part of a social setting for a prolonged period — working, eating, partying, attending religious services
→Goal: understand the people (norms, values, behavior) in the study — “Deep hanging out” (Rosaldo, 1994)

What is observation?

→Can be episodic (less in depth) — not necessarily immersive (e.g., observe spaces and places by visiting briefly)

When to Use Ethnography or Observation

→New topics where little is known
→Important differences between insiders and outsiders, especially those obscured from public view
→Exploratory studies — deep insight into contexts, relationships, behaviors
→Uniquely rich insights into gaps between stated and revealed preferences

Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

•“Thick description” of complex phenomena
•Range of perspectives among group (heterogeneity)
•High internal validity (when done well) = deep insight

Disadvantages

•Protracted — can take a very long time to earn trust
•Difficult: highly ethnographer-dependent
•Ethics & bias always concerns
•Low external validity (usually non-random sampling)

Key Considerations

Sampling

Deep analysis of one group or place, but not necessarily representative or generalizable

Veracity

Does the presence of a researcher change the behavior of the study population?

Reliability

Did the researcher understand what they think they saw and heard? Insider vs. outsider perspective?

Policy-Relevant Ethnography

Ethics and Observation

Respect for Persons

Individuals should be treated as autonomous agents; persons with diminished autonomy are entitled to protection

Beneficence

Do no harm; maximize possible benefits and minimize possible harms to subjects

Justice

Fair distribution of the burdens and benefits of research; who bears the risks and who receives the benefits?

Ethical Considerations for Your Assignment

→Public settings only — in ethnographies of private settings, obtain consent from individuals/groups being studied
→Anonymize data — field notes and memo should not contain identifying information unless essential and consent has been given
→Non-intervention — as much as possible, observe the natural course of events
→Sensitivity to context — some behaviors might be considered more private, even in a public setting

What Is “Thick Description”?

→Show rather than tell — use evocative language when describing a field site
→Be as specific as possible — avoid general or abstract words
→Use examples, descriptions, and quotations to make the experience concrete for the reader
Adapted from Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

W
Who, What, When, Where, How

T

Moving from Vague to “Thick”

Too vague Thicker description
“Coach Rodriguez was my favorite.” “Coach Rodriguez was the only coach who spent 30 minutes working one-on-one with each athlete every week.”
“The chair of the meeting was ineffective.” “The chair addressed only 3 of 5 points on the agenda in the 60 minutes allotted for the meeting.”
Adapted from Vanderbilt (2012)
21
EthnographyExamplesEthicsField NotesAssignment #2

Avoiding Conclusive Statements

Too conclusive Thicker description
“The teacher likes the students.” “The teacher smiles when the students enter the room, greets them attentively and warmly, and hugs those that run to her.”
“The interviewee was uncomfortable with this line of questioning.” “During questions 5 thru 7 the interviewee started fidgeting in his seat, touching his hand to his mouth, and speaking more slowly while clearing his throat repeatedly. He displayed none of those behaviors in the first 4 questions.”
Adapted from Vanderbilt (2012)

What Could Be vs. What Is Happening

Too vague Thicker description
“Students will learn math better with hands-on examples than numbers on a chalkboard.” “Yesterday, when the teacher wrote problems on the board, 3 students answered all problems correctly. Today, when the teacher used blocks and bottle caps, 8 students answered most problems correctly in under 30 seconds.”
“The interviewee was uncomfortable with this line of questioning.” “During questions 5 thru 7 the interviewee started fidgeting in his seat, touching his hand to his mouth, and speaking more slowly while clearing his throat repeatedly. He displayed none of those behaviors in the first 4 questions.”
Adapted from Vanderbilt (2012)

Example Field Notes

6:29 PM — While the cashier bills the customers, a family enters the store. Presumably, the husband, his wife in a shalwar kameez outfit and their two children — a boy and a girl, both of whom seem like 7–8 years old. The kids are effervescent and bubbly as soon as they enter the store, running in between the aisles as though they know the place well. The parents make their way to the oil/ghee section, and they start discussing which brand is the most cost-effective.
6:33 PM — The parents continue to go through the aisles, picking up specific spices, pickles, rice and legumes. Intermittently, the children grab snacks from the snack aisle. The parents emphatically refuse most of the snacks but allow both children to choose one brand of fried snack they can mutually decide.
6:37 PM — The family go through the store almost mechanically. They speak in Hindi and tell the cashier to extend a hello to his father. The cashier then lets the children choose one candy each — they happily choose a tamarind lollipop each.
— Abeera Ahmed · Foodland, Cambridge, MA · Sunday, March 5, 2023

Example Field Notes

The room is a spacious auditorium with wood paneled floors covered in a fading finish dotted with folding tables for people to congregate. I am standing at the front of the room near the door at the top of the staircase ushering people towards the food line and answering any questions.

The style of dress is not that different from what you might see at the typical Sunday mass: most people dressed in modest, casual clothing with weather appropriate jackets. Prominent urban streetwear brands like Jordan and True Religion are some of the most common styles.

Most people seeking assistance were women — either alone or with children — as well as some two parent family units and the occasional single man. Children skewed younger in age with most looking to be in early adolescence roughly 10 years old and below. While the Church welcomes anyone, there were no unaccompanied minors or single young people.

— Jaime Cobham · St. Athanasius Church, Brooklyn · Sunday, March 5, 2023

Assignment #2: Observation

Part 1: Field Notes

Observe a social location for at least 60 minutes. During your visit, unobtrusively write short notes.

Part 2: Reflection Memo

Write a reflection memo (~250 words) surfacing key themes and analytic observations.

Part 3: Cover Note

Write a cover note (~250 words): where & when, why you chose this location, markers or themes related to your policy puzzle.

Be Creative (but Safe) in Choosing Your Site

EXAMPLE REFLECTION MEMO

The greatest themes I saw in my observation was not from the protestors themselves but from the people walking through them. Men passing through seemed more likely to make loud jokes; the women walking through consistently kept their eyes to the ground as if to avoid inviting attention.

From this observation, even if protest movements do not interact with women seeking services, their presence would likely deter women from seeking in-clinic care. In areas where protestors are more likely, women may be more likely to pursue telehealth abortions.
