Canonical Example

Moving to Opportunity

—A landmark randomized housing mobility experiment sponsored by HUD (N=4,604 low-income families assigned vouchers to move to higher-opportunity neighborhoods).

—The quantitative evaluation found significant long-term gains: improved physical health, mental health (with differential gender impacts among youth), and increased college attendance and earnings for children exposed young (but null or negative effects for older teens).

Core mechanisms uncovered by qualitative MTO research:
stress reduction via safety; gendered adaptation to new environments; norm and expectation transmission; loss and reconfiguration of social capital; institutional friction and discrimination; developmental timing (childhood exposure matters most).

The numbers told you whether relocation changed outcomes, and the interviews helped explain how and why.

HUD, Moving to Opportunity Final Impacts Report (2011) · Chetty, Hendren & Katz, The Effects of Exposure to Better Neighborhoods on Children (2016)

Canonical Example

U.S. Census & Hard-to-Count Populations

—The Census Bureau knew that certain racial and ethnic groups were being undercounted, but not why.

—Ethnographic studies placed researchers in 29 communities, where they discovered the mechanisms: complex living arrangements made "household" definitions meaningless; distrust of government motivated nonresponse, etc.

Administrative systems often fail precisely where they know the least.
Qualitative work helped the state redesign outreach, communication, and field operations for the 2000 and subsequent censuses: rewritten household roster instructions, bilingual and community-based enumerators, questionnaire assistance at community locations, and expanded local partnerships.

The numbers described what was being missed, the ethnographies helped inform why and how to fix it.

GAO, Ethnographic Studies Can Inform Agencies' Actions (2003) · NRC, Principles and Practices for a Federal Statistical Agency · U.S. Census Bureau, Understanding Undercounted Populations (2023)

Canonical Example

WHO Guidelines for a Positive Childbirth Experience

—WHO childbirth guidelines historically relied on clinical trial data measuring mortality, but facility-based births were declining even as survival outcomes improved. So why did women continue to avoid birth facilities?

—Downe et al. (2018) synthesized 35 qualitative studies across 19 countries, capturing the voices of 1,800+ women. Bohren et al. (2015) documented systematic mistreatment across 65 studies in 34 countries. Women revealed they wanted not just survival, but dignity, agency, companionship, and kind care.

The first major WHO guideline to formally integrate qualitative evidence alongside RCTs, using the CERQual framework.
Redefined success from "mother and baby survive" to a "positive childbirth experience."

RCTs could measure whether interventions reduced mortality, while qualitative research helped reveal why women avoided facilities and what "working" actually meant to the people affected.

Downe et al., What Matters to Women (PLOS ONE, 2018) · WHO Intrapartum Care Guidelines (2018)

Learning Objectives for This Course

01 Understand the Craft

Develop critical evaluation skills to discern high-quality vs. low-quality qualitative research

Formalize qualitative reasoning skills (mechanisms, integrate qual evidence with quant causal inference...)

02 Practical Skills

Familiarize you with foundational qualitative approaches & their complementary strengths & weaknesses

Experiential learning: observation (ethnographic methods), interview, focus group, archives

03 Ethics

Understand the ethical implications of qual research, including considerations related to consent, confidentiality, and representation

04 Strategic Thinking

Consider how qualitative insights can enhance strategy formulation and policy innovation

Explore how quant + qual can be complementary (mixed methods)

05 Capstone Project

The final will serve as a bridge to your PAE (and maybe your summer internship): propose a project related to a real-world policy issue/client

06 AI and Qual Methods

State-of-the-art exposure to AI applications in qualitative research (how AI can support data collection, transcription, coding, exploratory analysis, workflows, transparency and reproducibility, pattern detection... while critically assessing its limitations, biases, and ethical risks)

Different Policy Areas Value Different Methods

Avey et al. (2022) Survey of 616 officials in several dozen agencies in 2017–18, 17% response rate.
•Most skeptical of abstract and technical methods.
•Highest confidence in contextual judgment: area studies, ethnography, history.
•Uses research more as framework than as direct instrument.
•Security stands apart from trade and development in methodological taste.
Source: Avey et al. (2022) close approximation; replication data unavailable on Dataverse

GenAI & This Course

Course AI Policy
•We will experiment with LLMs for coding, pattern-finding, and interpreting qualitative data
•OK: AI-powered search, exploring ideas, refining thinking, understanding course material
•Not OK: Submitting text produced predominantly by AI (treat it like copying from a source)
•You are responsible for accuracy of all work you submit
•Think of AI as a tireless one-on-one tutor: use it to help you learn, not to do the work for you
AI for Feedback & Assessment
•We may experiment with AI to assist grading (always with full transparency)
•For example, other courses have used Pensieve (FERPA-compliant), which compares answers against a rubric, then humans review every response and assign the final score
•Another potential use: normalizing grades across the cohort to reduce variance and bias depending on which teaching team member graded your work
•Final determination is always made by a human (we will always discuss these practices openly)
The Bottom Line
•Domain knowledge matters more, not less, in an AI-rich world
•AI agents can execute tasks, but effective supervision requires strong mental models of the underlying subject
•For this course: you need a solid understanding of what makes good qualitative research to get maximum value from AI tools
•Without that expertise, you can't spot errors, ask the right questions, or direct AI effectively
Read more: Teddy Svoronos, "Agentic Everything"

Quant vs. Qual

Quantitative

Research that relies on measuring variables using a numerical system, analyzing using statistical models, and reporting relationships among variables.

Qualitative

Approaches used to explore and interpret human behavior through non-numerical data. Emphasizes depth, context, and subjective meaning rather than statistical generalization.

Examples of difficult-to-quantify concepts that most agree exist: power, trust, status, solidarity, culture, resilience, fear, political will, legitimacy, accountability, public confidence, credibility, etc.

•Estimate distributions, patterns, and averages
•Make predictions

When Qualitative?

•Questions about experience, meaning, and processes
•HOW and WHY questions
•Data not reducible to counting or measuring
•Want to understand behavior from informant's perspective

Two Approaches to Mixed Methods

Mixed Data Collection

Combine two or more kinds of data

Example: Geoinformatics + Fieldwork and Interviews

Mixed Data Analysis

Two or more analytical strategies on the same data

Example: Quantitative text analysis + Archival/content analysis

When Are Mixed Methods Valuable?

Confirmation (Triangulation)

  • →Verify findings from one type of data with another type
  • →Conflicting findings can be generative — e.g., Pager (2005) audit study of employment discrimination

OverviewReal WorldLogisticsQual/QuantAssignment #1

When Are Mixed Methods Valuable?

Complementarity

  • →One type of data can compensate for weaknesses of another
  • →Examples: Cohen (2016) on wartime sexual violence; Han, McKenna, Oyakaya (2021) on social movement power-building
