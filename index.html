Canonical Example

Moving to Opportunity

A landmark randomized experiment relocated families from high-poverty public housing to low-poverty neighborhoods. The quantitative evaluation found mental health improved for women and girls, but not for boys. No employment gains were detected.

A companion qualitative study (Popkin et al., 2002) interviewed hundreds of families and revealed why: mothers described escaping constant gunfire and sexual predation. Girls gained freedom of movement; boys stayed tied to old neighborhood networks and gangs. Safety, not economic opportunity, was the mechanism.

The numbers told you whether relocation changed outcomes. The interviews told you how safety, stress, social networks, and parenting actually mediated those changes.

Popkin, Harris & Cunningham, Families in Transition (HUD, 2002) · Orr et al., MTO Interim Impacts (HUD, 2003)

Canonical Example

U.S. Census & Hard-to-Count Populations

The Census Bureau knew that certain racial and ethnic groups were being undercounted, but not why. Ethnographic studies placed researchers in 29 communities, where they discovered the mechanisms: women concealed adult males on forms to protect welfare benefits; complex living arrangements made "household" definitions meaningless; distrust of government motivated nonresponse.

These findings directly changed Census operations: rewritten household roster instructions, bilingual and community-based enumerators, questionnaire assistance at community locations, and expanded local partnerships. The Bureau's own staff called this culture shift "an important result of the ethnographic studies."

Administrative systems often fail precisely where they know the least. Qualitative work helped the state redesign outreach, communication, and field operations for the 2000 and subsequent censuses.

GAO, Ethnographic Studies Can Inform Agencies' Actions (2003) · NRC, Principles and Practices for a Federal Statistical Agency

Canonical Example

WHO Guidelines for a Positive Childbirth Experience

WHO childbirth guidelines historically relied on clinical trial data measuring mortality. But facility-based births were declining in some settings even as survival outcomes improved. Why?

Downe et al. (2018) synthesized 35 qualitative studies across 19 countries, capturing the voices of 1,800+ women. Bohren et al. (2015) documented systematic mistreatment across 65 studies in 34 countries. Women revealed they wanted not just survival, but dignity, agency, companionship, and kind care.

This was the first major WHO guideline to formally integrate qualitative evidence alongside RCTs, using the CERQual framework. It redefined success from "mother and baby survive" to a "positive childbirth experience."

RCTs could measure whether interventions reduced mortality. Only qualitative research could reveal why women avoided facilities and what "working" actually meant to the people affected.

Downe et al., What Matters to Women (PLOS ONE, 2018) · WHO Intrapartum Care Guidelines (2018)

Increased Policy Use

Ethnography: qualitative research method used to understand the culture and practices of a group, community or organization, often through participation, close observation, and interviewing

GAO Report (2003): Federal agencies using ethnographic studies include Commerce, Defense, HHS, HUD, and more.

Quant vs. Qual

Quantitative

Research that relies on measuring variables using a numerical system, analyzing using statistical models, and reporting relationships among variables.

Qualitative

Approaches used to explore and interpret human behavior through non-numerical data. Emphasizes depth, context, and subjective meaning rather than statistical generalization.

32
OverviewIntrosReal WorldLogisticsMixed MethodsAssignment #1

When Quantitative?

  • 01Estimate distributions, patterns, and averages
  • 02Make predictions
  • 03Test causal relationships
  • 04Generalize results to wider populations
33
OverviewIntrosReal WorldLogisticsMixed MethodsAssignment #1

When Qualitative?

  • 01Questions about experience, meaning, and processes
  • 02HOW and WHY questions
  • 03Data not reducible to counting or measuring
  • 04Want to understand behavior from informant's perspective

Two Approaches to Mixed Methods

Mixed Data Collection

Combine two or more kinds of data

Example: Geoinformatics + Fieldwork and Interviews

Mixed Data Analysis

Two or more analytical strategies on the same data

Example: Quantitative text analysis + Archival/content analysis

When Are Mixed Methods Valuable?

Confirmation (Triangulation)

  • →Verify findings from one type of data with another type
  • →Conflicting findings can be generative — e.g., Pager (2005) audit study of employment discrimination
38
OverviewIntrosReal WorldLogisticsMixed MethodsAssignment #1

When Are Mixed Methods Valuable?

Complementarity

  • →One type of data can compensate for weaknesses of another
  • →Examples: Cohen (2016) on wartime sexual violence; Han, McKenna, Oyakaya (2021) on social movement power-building
