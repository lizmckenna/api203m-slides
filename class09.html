What are focus groups?

Features

Heise & Ellsberg (2005)

  • •Group of ~6–10 individuals selected because they share characteristics relevant to a defined area of interest
  • •Moderated discussion
  • •Note-takers & recording (with consent)
  • •Question protocol AND activities

Definition

“A research technique that collects data through group interaction on a topic determined by the researcher.”

— Morgan 1996, 130, quoted in Cyr

04
DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Characteristics of focus groups

  • 01Social — data emerges through group interaction
  • 02Privileges the perspectives of participants — not the researcher's framing
  • 03Can be analyzed at three levels: individual, group, and the interaction itself

Cyr 2019

05
DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Why use a focus group?

  • →Explore norms, beliefs, practices, language at the level of the group (vs. individuals)
  • →Assess needs, develop interventions
  • →Test new ideas/programs
  • →Improve existing programs
  • →Generate ideas — help construct a survey, begin research where little is known

Heise & Ellsberg (2005)

06
DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Most useful when…

  • •The topic is a socially constructed phenomenon
  • •You want to understand group processes
  • •You want to know how people think and act in social groups
  • •The topic is sensitive or stigmatized
  • •The topic involves vulnerable or marginalized groups
  • •You need to make sure concepts make sense in context
  • •Little is known about the topic or possible answers

Cyr, Chapter 2

Origins of focus groups

Office of War Information, 1940s

The OWI contracts sociologists Robert K. Merton and Paul Lazarsfeld to use social science methods to gauge public opinion about war propaganda.

  • →How were messages being processed and mediated by different social groups?
  • →Focus groups developed as a way to study nuanced, in-depth responses in a social context

Logistics

DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Logistics to plan in advance

Setting

  • •Quiet, private, accessible space
  • •Seating in a circle or around a table
  • •Recording equipment + backup
  • •Consent forms, name tents, materials for activities

Roles

  • •Moderator — runs the discussion
  • •Note-taker — captures dynamics, body language
  • •Scribe — records key quotes & activity outputs
  • •Refreshments & incentives where appropriate

Recruitment

20
DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Leveraging focus groups: composition

Pick people who will have answers to your questions

E.g., if your puzzle is about how people develop party identification, recruit strong-identifying Democrats and Republicans — not independents.

Use homogeneity and heterogeneity strategically

E.g., to research solutions to campus sexual violence, separate by gender to encourage discussion but vary major, year, and SES for breadth.

Consider acquaintances vs. strangers

E.g., will picking people from the same program make them more or less likely to share their true opinions on its efficacy?

Adapted from 2023 TFs (Cat O'Donnell, Jessica Van Meir)

DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Three reasons to gather people together

A response to social desirability & groupthink

Group dynamics can surface, not just suppress, what people really think.

Meaning making is a group-level process

People often work out what they think in dialogue with others.

Potential for collective empowerment

Participants can build solidarity and political voice through the encounter.

DefinitionsPracticalitiesModeratingThursday

Guiding principles of moderating

  • →Be interested in participants — show “positive regard”
  • →Be a moderator, not a participant
  • →Be ready to hear unpleasant views
  • →You can't moderate all groups — know your limits
  • →Use your unique talents

Morgan and Krueger (1997)
