Key Considerations

Sampling

Deep analysis of one group/place, but not necessarily representative or generalizable

Veracity

Does the presence of a researcher change the behavior of the study population?

Reliability

Did the researcher understand what they think they saw and heard? Is the researcher an insider or an outsider? How might this affect interpretation?

Nuha Muntasser

Artificial Intelligence Student Safety Team Event

“At 7:10 PM, the group filters into a conference room at the back of the office that is clearly designed for fewer people than are now in it. Over twenty-five attendees fill a space that should only hold about fourteen, with several people leaning against the walls for the entire event, visibly holding either their paper plates or phones. The ceiling is industrial, with exposed blue pipes running overhead, and a large window behind my seat looks out over Church Street, where the last pale light of the evening is fading behind the brick buildings and the still-glowing Dunkin’ sign shining bright…”

“The speaker joins via Zoom, his face appearing on a floor to ceiling projected screen in front of the conference table. Given the size of the room and the limitations of the host’s laptop camera, the speaker notes that he cannot see all of the attendees in the room. He looks younger than his credentials suggest, speaking from what appears to be a home office, and at one point apologizes for any background noise due to his children screaming in the background. Nobody in the room reacts visibly to this. I settled into a seat near the back behind most of the attendees.”

Emma Buelte

Shaw Roxbury Branch, Boston Public Library

“Do you go to the law school?”
“Huh?”
The staff member from the health checkup, the one with the man bun, has walked up to me. “No, sorry.”
“Harvard, right? We’re from Harvard too. I saw your sweater.”

Now it clicks. When I rushed out of the door for an exam this morning, I just grabbed the next best hoodie off my chair, with big crimson “HARVARD” letters over the chest. It was only when I approached the library that I deeply regretted my choice – way to be indiscreet. I loosely threw a big scarf around my neck that I thought covered the logo pretty well, though apparently not. I’m a bit embarrassed.

“Ahh, cool! Yeah. I go to the Kennedy School.”
“The what?”
“The policy school. It’s called Kennedy.”
“Ohhh, okay. Cool! Yeah I thought it was the law school…was gonna ask if you can give me some legal advice.”

I laugh. “Yeah, sorry, can’t offer that.”
He smiles, nods, and turns around to walk back to the Family Van stand.

Theory

What Is “Thick Description”?

Show, Don’t Tell

When we are thickly describing a field site, we are trying to show rather than tell by using evocative language.

Be Specific

The aim is to be as specific as possible by avoiding general or abstract words.

Make It Concrete

Rich field notes use anecdotes, examples, descriptions, and quotations to make the experience concrete for the reader.

Moving from Vague to “Thick”

Too vague / conclusiveThicker description
“The chair of the meeting was ineffective.” “The chair addressed only 3 of 5 points on the agenda in the 60 minutes allotted for the meeting.”
“The interviewee was uncomfortable with this line of questioning.” “During questions 5 thru 7 the interviewee started fidgeting in his seat, touching his hand to his mouth, and speaking more slowly while clearing his throat repeatedly. He displayed none of those behaviors in the first 4 questions.”

Adapted from Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

A Word on Learning Orientations…

Fixed Mindset

“I’m just not good at this.”

Avoids challenges, gives up easily, sees effort as fruitless

Growth Mindset

“I can learn to do this better.”

Embraces challenges, persists through setbacks, sees effort as path to mastery

Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (Carol Dweck, 2006)

Patterns, Connections, Contrasts

Pattern “In my observations, I noticed a general pattern of _________ as exemplified by _________, __________, and _________.”
Connection “Each time that ______________ occurred, _________________.”
Contrast “Although I generally observed ____________, ___________ stood out by ______________.”

DebriefWorkshopDescription → AnalysisLooking Forward

Inferences and Implications

Implication “One implication of what I observed could be __________________________.”
Theory → Evidence “One element of [insert concept/theory] involves _____________. This can be seen in [insert detail from field notes].”
Indirect observation “Although I did not observe X directly / explicitly, it is implied by ________________________.”

DebriefWorkshopDescription → AnalysisLooking Forward

Relate to Frameworks & Hypotheses

Surprise “At first I thought __________________________, but after conducting my research, what I found is that ___________________________.”
Test a hypothesis “The work of [insert scholar] hypothesizes _________________________. My fieldwork shows ___________________________.”
Confirm “In my fieldwork, I found ________________. This coincides with my [or someone else’s] hypothesis in that _________________.”
Challenge “It conflicts with my [or someone else’s] hypothesis in that _________________.”
