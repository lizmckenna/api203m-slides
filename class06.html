Key Considerations: Ethnographic Observation

  • →Sampling — Deep analysis of one group/place, but not necessarily representative or generalizable
  • →Veracity — Does the presence of a researcher change the behavior of the study population?
  • →Reliability — Did the researcher understand what they think they saw and heard? Is the researcher an insider or an outsider? How might this affect interpretation?

Example 1: Rebecca Lin

“A Chinese father and young boy, likely around 4 years old, stroll onto the playground. The father, who appears to be in his 40s and wears a blue UMass Sweatshirt and bright white Nikes, pulls a bright red wagon behind him. Inside the wagon lies a red spiderman blanket that looks soft to the touch. The young boy dressed in a bulky down jacket eagerly crawls through the orange and green hexagons that are significantly taller than his short stature. A shy smile covers the boy’s face and he intermittently giggles as he begins to crawl through the octagons. His dad stands guard by the wagon.

The boy slowly makes his way through the octagons and reaches the top of playground structure and he gleefully grins, waving down at his dad. Appearing satisfied with his conquest, the child quickly scurries down the structure, runs around it once, before hopping into the red wagon. The boy murmurs, ‘I want to go home,’ growing slowly louder and more insistent as his dad tries to put the spiderman blanket on him.”

Example 2: Tim Dobretsov

“Freddy is wearing a dark button-down shirt, though the top button of his shirt is unbuttoned. He is animated and confident as he shares his perspective on fighting dictatorship, vigorously using his hands to emphasize talking points. Towards the end of his opening remarks, which he delivers while shifting his gaze from one section of the audience to another, he concludes that a multidisciplinary approach is required to fight dictatorship and promote sustainable change…”

“An audible ‘recording in progress’ message from the computer interrupts Freddy as he wraps up, prompting laughter from the audience. This is the first explicit indication of the lighthearted mood that pervades the room. It becomes clear at this point that audience members remained engaged throughout Freddy’s presentation, perhaps due to his energetic and animated style. His speech was characterized by a certain urgency, though he did not raise his voice or speak quickly.”

Example 3: Kellie Lounds

“Another (presumably) mother is called to the lectern to give her registered public comment. She, too, wants to speak on the superintendent’s performance review. Very quickly, however, her comments take a different tone. She hurriedly disavows the leadership’s ‘authoritarian approach’ to school governance regarding the district’s ‘COVID injection mandate’ for participating in school sports; her tone starts to get more aggressive and I can feel the people around me shifting on either side.

What Is “Thick Description”?

  • →When we are thickly describing a field site, we are trying to show rather than tell by using evocative language.
  • →The aim is to be as specific as possible by avoiding general or abstract words.
  • →Rich field notes are ones that use anecdotes, examples, descriptions, and quotations to make the experience concrete for the reader.

Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

Moving from Vague to “Thick”

Too vagueThicker description
“Coach Rodriguez was my favorite.” “Coach Rodriguez was the only coach who spent 30 minutes working one-on-one with each athlete every week.”
“The chair of the meeting was ineffective.” “The chair addressed only 3 of 5 points on the agenda in the 60 minutes allotted for the meeting.”

Adapted from Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

Avoiding Conclusive Statements

Too conclusiveThicker description
“The teacher likes the students.” “The teacher smiles when the students enter the room, greets them attentively and warmly, and hugs those that run to her.”
“The interviewee was uncomfortable with this line of questioning.” “During questions 5 thru 7 the interviewee started fidgeting in his seat, touching his hand to his mouth, and speaking more slowly while clearing his throat repeatedly. He displayed none of those behaviors in the first 4 questions.”

Adapted from Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

What Could Be vs. What Is Happening

Too vagueThicker description
“Students will learn math better with hands-on examples than numbers on a chalkboard.” “Yesterday, when the teacher wrote problems on the board, 3 students answered all problems correctly. Today, when the teacher used blocks and bottle caps, 8 students answered most problems correctly in under 30 seconds.”

Adapted from Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

A Word on Learning Orientations…

Fixed Mindset

“I’m just not good at this.”

Avoids challenges, gives up easily, sees effort as fruitless

Growth Mindset

“I can learn to do this better.”

Embraces challenges, persists through setbacks, sees effort as path to mastery

Mindset: The New Psychology of Success (Carol Dweck, 2006)

Patterns, Connections, Contrasts

Pattern “In my observations, I noticed a general pattern of _________ as exemplified by _________, __________, and _________.”
Connection “Each time that ______________ occurred, _________________.”
Contrast “Although I generally observed ____________, ___________ stood out by ______________.”

Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

DebriefWorkshopDescription → AnalysisLooking Forward

Inferences and Implications

Implication “One implication of what I observed could be __________________________.”
Theory → Evidence “One element of [insert concept/theory] involves _____________. This can be seen in [insert detail from field notes].”
Indirect observation “Although I did not observe X directly / explicitly, it is implied by ________________________.”

Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

Relate to Frameworks & Hypotheses

Surprise “At first I thought __________________________, but after conducting my research, what I found is that ___________________________.”
Test a hypothesis “The work of [insert scholar] hypothesizes _________________________. My fieldwork shows ___________________________.”
Confirm “In my fieldwork, I found ________________. This coincides with my [or someone else’s] hypothesis in that _________________.”
Challenge “It conflicts with my [or someone else’s] hypothesis in that _________________.”

Vanderbilt Writing Studio (2012)

Making It Systematic: SSO

  • →Systematic Social Observation (SSO) — A structured approach to comparing observations across sites, times, or contexts
  • →How? — Use a consistent coding scheme to categorize what you observe (e.g., coding “in/out” groups, types of interactions, physical characteristics of spaces)

Baggetta and Bradenkamp (2021)
